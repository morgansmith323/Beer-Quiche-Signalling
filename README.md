Replication materials for a game-theoretic model of second-date decision-making, built on the Beer-Quiche signalling framework. The paper models the bill-split response as a credible signal of romantic interest and derives a two-zone Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium in which the signal is decision-relevant only above the 60th attractiveness percentile. This repository contains the Python code, Jupyter notebooks, and extended figure notes behind the paper's three computational appendix figures: a bifurcation surface mapping the zone boundary across key parameters, an expected utility gain landscape, and a 10,000-date Monte Carlo simulation validating the equilibrium structure.

Computational Visualisations and Analytical Extensions — Extended Technical Notes
This document is the extended companion to Appendix B of the paper. It provides full technical motivation, implementation detail, and interpretive commentary for the three computational figures. It is intended for readers who wish to reproduce, extend, or critique the visualisations, and is housed in the repository rather than the paper to avoid length constraints.
B.1 Figure V1 — Bifurcation Surface: Sensitivity of p to (k, e)*
Overview and Construction
Figure V1 maps the zone boundary p* across an 80×80 grid of the loss-aversion steepness parameter k ∈ [5, 22] and the embarrassment cost e ∈ [10, 35], with the canonical parameterisation (k = 13, e = 20) marked explicitly. The left panel presents this as a full heatmap with iso-p* contour lines, and the right panel slices the surface at three fixed values of e (e = 15, 20, 28) to isolate the effect of k alone. For each (k, e) pair, p* is computed via bisection on the condition μ(I | R, p) = q*(p), using a tolerance of 1×10⁻⁶. Grid points for which no separating equilibrium exists, because α(p) never rises steeply enough to drain the rejection pool, are rendered as the dark region in the lower-left of the heatmap.
Key Findings
The figure's primary finding is that p* is substantially more sensitive to k than to e. Reducing k from 13 to 7, which weakens the loss-aversion mechanism governing how rapidly α(p) rises with attractiveness, shifts the zone boundary from p* ≈ 0.60 to p* ≈ 0.88, a displacement of nearly 0.30 across the attractiveness distribution. An equivalent proportional reduction in e moves p* by less than 0.05. This asymmetry is theoretically significant: it confirms that the model's screening efficiency is driven primarily by the behavioural mechanism, specifically prospect-theoretic loss aversion shaping the type I woman's acceptance behaviour, rather than by the raw payoff parameters.
The dark region in the lower-left of the heatmap identifies the parameter combinations under which no separating equilibrium exists, because α(p) never rises steeply enough to drain the rejection pool of interested types. This region is bounded by a contour that is steep in the k-direction and shallow in the e-direction, consistent with the asymmetric sensitivity result.
Robustness Interpretation
The figure serves a robustness function. Because the canonical parameterisation sits well within the separating-equilibrium region and away from both the no-equilibrium boundary and the p* = 1 ceiling, the two-zone result is not a knife-edge consequence of a particular calibration. Moderate perturbations to either k or e preserve the qualitative structure of the equilibrium. The iso-p* contours in the heatmap panel demonstrate that the direction of maximum sensitivity runs almost entirely along the k-axis, which means empirical uncertainty about e has limited implications for the model's qualitative predictions. The fixed-e slices in the right panel make this directional asymmetry legible in a way the heatmap alone does not: the three curves are tightly clustered relative to the displacement induced by equivalent variation in k.
Reproduction Notes
The bisection procedure for each grid point initialises with bounds [0.01, 0.99] and converges in under 30 iterations at the stated tolerance. The no-equilibrium region is identified by checking whether the sign of μ(I | R, p) − q*(p) is constant across the full [0, 1] domain before bisection; if so, p* is recorded as undefined and the cell is rendered black. All computations are performed in Python using NumPy vectorisation across the grid.

B.2 Figure V2 — Expected Utility Gain Landscape
Overview and Construction
Figure V2 visualises the payoff gain from optimal signal-conditioning relative to the naïve always-propose strategy, defined as ΔEU = EU(signal-optimal) − EU(always-propose), across the full (p, k) parameter space. The left panel renders this as a three-dimensional surface with the canonical k = 13 slice highlighted in red, and the right panel projects the same data as a heatmap overlaid with the analytically derived p* boundary and the Zone 1/Zone 2 partition. The surface is computed on a 100×100 grid of (p, k) values, with ΔEU evaluated at each point by computing both the signal-optimal and naïve expected utilities and taking their difference.
The signal-optimal strategy is defined as: propose if and only if μ(I | s) ≥ q*(p). The naïve strategy proposes regardless of signal. Expected utility under each strategy is computed by integrating over the signal distribution induced by α(p), β(p), and π(p), weighting proposal and non-proposal payoffs by posterior belief and complement respectively.
Key Findings
The surface makes two contributions that the paper's analytical results do not make as directly. First, it shows that the payoff gain from conditioning on the bill signal is essentially zero throughout Zone 1, the region below p*, regardless of k. This confirms that the naïve strategy is not merely suboptimal below p* but is approximately costless to follow, providing an intuitive justification for why the pooling equilibrium in Zone 1 is stable. A man operating below p* loses almost nothing by ignoring the signal entirely.
Second, the surface demonstrates that the gain from signal-conditioning is not uniform across Zone 2 but accelerates with both p and k. At the canonical parameterisation and p = 0.65, the numerical verification in Appendix A.4 establishes a gain of +7.17 payoff units from correctly reading a rejection signal. Figure V2 shows that this figure rises sharply as p increases further beyond p*, and rises further still as k increases, because a steeper α(p) more rapidly empties the rejection pool of interested types, making each additional unit of signal informativeness more consequential.
Zone Boundary Cross-Validation
The heatmap panel makes the zone partition immediately legible: the boundary between the dark and bright regions of the ΔEU surface coincides precisely with the analytically derived p* curve. This constitutes a visual cross-validation of the bisection result in Appendix A.3 that is entirely independent of it. The transition in the payoff surface is not computed from p* but emerges from the simulation of ΔEU directly, and their alignment therefore provides independent confirmation that the bisection threshold correctly identifies the point at which signal-conditioning becomes payoff-relevant.
Reproduction Notes
The canonical k = 13 slice on the 3D surface is rendered using Matplotlib's plot_surface with a red curve overlaid at the fixed k index closest to 13. The heatmap uses a masked array to suppress the no-equilibrium region (where p* is undefined) and overlays the p* boundary as a white dashed contour extracted from the V1 bisection grid. The canonical parameterisation point (p = 0.65, k = 13) is marked as a pink circle at its exact grid coordinates.

B.3 Figure V3 — Monte Carlo Screening Efficiency
Overview and Construction
Figure V3 presents the results of a 10,000-date simulation in which male attractiveness is drawn from a Beta(2.2, 2.2) distribution, a realistic bell-shaped prior over [0, 1], and outcomes are tracked across the full range of k values. For each simulated date, the woman's type is drawn from a Bernoulli with probability π(p), her signal is drawn conditional on type from Bernoulli(α(p)) or Bernoulli(β(p)) respectively, and the man's decision is computed under both the optimal signal-conditioning strategy and the naïve always-propose strategy. The four panels report: (a) precision, recall, and F₁-score against k; (b) the posterior distribution μ(I|s) by signal and type at k = 13; (c) precision-recall curves as e is swept from 8 to 35 under three guilt regimes; and (d) a net match-rate gain heatmap across (p, k) bins.
Panel (a): Aggregate Screening Metrics
At k = 13, the optimal signal-conditioning strategy achieves an F₁-score of approximately 0.84, against a naïve baseline of approximately 0.72, with the gap widening monotonically as k increases. This confirms that the model's screening benefit is empirically meaningful under realistic distributional assumptions and does not depend on attractiveness being concentrated at the high end of the distribution where the signal is most informative. The precision and recall curves diverge in a theoretically consistent direction: the optimal strategy gains precision (fewer false proposals) at no cost to recall below p*, because the signal is uninformative there, and gains both precision and recall above p* by correctly filtering rejection signals.
Panel (b): Posterior Distribution by Signal and Type
Panel (b) is particularly instructive for understanding why the bill-split functions as a credible signal. The posterior distributions for type I and type U women are well-separated conditional on the Accept signal, with the type I Accept mass concentrated well above q*, but substantially overlap conditional on the Reject signal in Zone 1. It is only in Zone 2, where k has pushed α(p) above approximately 0.808, that the Reject distributions separate sufficiently for the signal to be decision-relevant. The dashed vertical line at q* = 0.507 marks the decision threshold at the simulation's average attractiveness draw, and the distribution mass on either side of it illustrates the frequency of correct and incorrect decisions under each strategy. This panel provides a distributional analogue of the analytical single-crossing condition discussed in Section 2.
Panel (c): Precision-Recall Trade-off Across Embarrassment Cost
Panel (c) traces precision-recall curves as the embarrassment cost e is varied from 8 to 35, revealing a meaningful trade-off: higher e improves precision (the man is less likely to propose to an uninterested woman) at the cost of recall (he becomes more likely to miss genuinely interested women). This occurs because higher e raises q*(p), shifting the threshold upward and making the man more conservative. The naïve strategy appears as a single point well below the optimal frontier across all k values, confirming that it is Pareto-dominated by the signal-conditioning strategy for any combination of e and k that produces a separating equilibrium. The three guilt regimes (e = 7, 13, 20) trace curves of visibly different curvature, with higher guilt shifting the frontier toward precision and compressing the recall range, consistent with the theoretical prediction that e operates as a selector on the man's conservatism rather than on the signal's informativeness.
Panel (d): Net Match-Rate Gain by (p, k) Bin
Panel (d) closes the argument by providing simulation-based confirmation of the two-zone partition that is entirely independent of the closed-form derivations in Appendix A. The net match-rate gain surface is concentrated in a sharp band immediately above p*, with near-zero gain throughout Zone 1 and rapidly increasing gain as p rises into Zone 2. The p* boundary overlaid on the heatmap aligns closely with the transition from the near-zero green region to the positive-gain yellow-green region, demonstrating that the analytical threshold and the simulated screening benefit coincide across the full range of k values tested. The near-zero gain in Zone 1 is not exactly zero due to simulation noise from the finite 10,000-date sample, but the mean gain across Zone 1 bins is below 0.005 match-rate units at all k values, well within the simulation's standard error.
Reproduction Notes
The simulation is seeded for reproducibility (seed = 42). The Beta(2.2, 2.2) distribution was selected to produce a bell-shaped prior with moderate variance (σ² ≈ 0.054) and no probability mass at the extremes, reflecting the empirical prior that dates do not occur exclusively at the tails of the attractiveness distribution. All four panels are generated from a single simulation pass to ensure internal consistency of the posterior distributions across panels. The (p, k) heatmap in panel (d) bins attractiveness into 20 equal-width intervals and k into 18 values from 5 to 22, with colour representing mean match-rate gain within each bin across all simulated dates falling in that bin.

Summary
Taken together, the three figures establish three propositions that complement the paper's algebraic results. The bifurcation surface confirms that the equilibrium structure is robust to parameter perturbation and that k is the dominant determinant of screening efficiency. The EU gain landscape shows that the value of signal-conditioning is concentrated precisely where the model predicts it should be, and is negligible where the model predicts the signal is uninformative. The Monte Carlo simulation demonstrates that these analytical results translate into measurable improvements in matching accuracy under realistic empirical conditions, providing external validation of the calibration choices made throughout the model.
